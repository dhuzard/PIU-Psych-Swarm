# Smartphone Overuse / Problematic Smartphone Use — Clinical Picture

## Nosological Status

Problematic smartphone use (PSU) has no formal diagnostic status in ICD-11 or DSM-5. It is an
umbrella research construct covering excessive, uncontrolled smartphone use that causes functional
impairment. Related terms include:

- **Smartphone addiction** — widely used in self-report scales; implies a harder nosological claim
  than the evidence currently supports.
- **Nomophobia** — fear of being without a mobile phone; dimensional measure of smartphone
  dependency, not a separate disorder.
- **PSU** — preferred neutral term in recent literature (Billieux et al. 2015).
- **Phone-compulsive use** — emphasises compulsive checking behaviour distinct from duration.

Always specify which construct a study addresses; scales differ substantially in what they measure.

## Assessment Instruments

- **Smartphone Addiction Scale (SAS):** Kwon et al. 2013; 33 items; 6 subscales; validated in
  Korean and English; widely used but criticised for conflating heavy use with disorder.
- **Smartphone Addiction Scale – Short Version (SAS-SV):** 10 items; cut-off ≥31 (males) /
  ≥33 (females) proposed by Kwon et al. 2013; frequently applied across cultures.
- **Problematic Mobile Phone Use Questionnaire (PMPUQ):** Billieux et al. 2008; captures
  prohibited use, dangerous use, and dependence subscales separately.
- **Mobile Phone Problem Use Scale (MPPUS):** Bianchi & Phillips 2005; 27-item scale.
- **Key caveat:** Cut-off scores were derived from convenience samples; clinical validation is weak.

## Prevalence Estimates

- Prevalence estimates vary from 10% to 40% in adolescent and university samples depending on
  instrument and cut-off — treat all figures with caution.
- Khoury et al. (2021, meta-analysis): pooled prevalence ~27% in adolescents (SAS-SV based studies).
- Lopez-Fernandez et al. (2017): cross-national study (7 countries); higher PSU in younger adults
  and females; cultural context moderates the association.
- University students: high-risk group given autonomy and 24/7 device access.

## Clinical Features

- **Compulsive checking:** repeated, unplanned checking of notifications unrelated to task demands.
- **Displaced sleep:** device use in bed delaying sleep onset; associated with poorer sleep quality.
- **Physical symptoms:** text neck, digital eye strain, repetitive strain (thumb/wrist).
- **Attentional interference:** phone presence (even face-down) reduces available working memory
  capacity (Ward et al. 2017 "brain drain" effect).
- **Mood regulation:** phubbing (phone snubbing in social settings) driven by anxiety management.

## Comorbidity Profile

- Anxiety: strongest psychiatric correlate; especially social anxiety and fear of negative evaluation.
- Depression: moderate association; possibly mediated through passive consumption, social comparison.
- Sleep: smartphone use near bedtime disrupts sleep onset via melatonin suppression and cognitive
  arousal; bidirectional — poor sleep also increases next-day PSU.
- ADHD: attention dysregulation increases vulnerability to compulsive checking cycles.

## Diagnostic Cautions

- Smartphones are multi-purpose tools; high use for work, navigation, or communication may be
  functional, not disordered. Content and context of use matter more than total duration.
- Adolescent PSU should be distinguished from developmentally normative peer-communication patterns.
- "Digital detox" interventions lack RCT evidence; do not over-medicalise moderate use.

## Key References

### Billieux et al. 2015
Billieux J, Maurage P, Lopez-Fernandez O, Kuss DJ, Griffiths MD. Can disordered mobile phone use be
considered a behavioral addiction? An update on current evidence and a comprehensive model for future
research. Curr Addict Rep. 2015;2(2):156-162. doi:10.1007/s40429-015-0054-y.
Summary: Conceptual review proposing four PSU pathways: impulsive, extraversion-related, relationship
avoidance, and cyberaddiction; argues against single-syndrome model.

### Kwon et al. 2013
Kwon M, et al. Development and validation of a smartphone addiction scale (SAS). PLoS ONE.
2013;8(2):e56936. doi:10.1371/journal.pone.0056936. PMID: 23468893.
Summary: Developed SAS and SAS-SV; 6 subscales; validated in Korean high-school and university sample.

### Lopez-Fernandez et al. 2017
Lopez-Fernandez O, et al. Problematic smartphone use in an international sample of young people
across 11 countries. Comput Human Behav. 2017;75:210-220. doi:10.1016/j.chb.2017.05.004.
Summary: Cross-national differences in PSU; females and younger users score higher; cultural context
moderates relationships with mental health.

### Ward et al. 2017
Ward AF, Duke K, Gneezy A, Bos MW. Brain drain: the mere presence of one's own smartphone reduces
available cognitive capacity. J Assoc Consum Res. 2017;2(2):140-154. doi:10.1086/691462.
Summary: Phone presence (even face-down, switched off) reduced working memory capacity; effect
stronger in participants with higher smartphone dependence.

### Khoury et al. 2021
Khoury JM, et al. Prevalence of problematic smartphone use in different populations: meta-analysis.
J Addict Dis. 2021;39(3):296-307. doi:10.1080/10550887.2021.1894474.
Summary: Pooled prevalence ~26.8% using SAS-SV; higher in females; substantial heterogeneity
across studies confirms that cut-off choice drives the estimate.
