# Mechanisms and Neurocognition in Problematic Internet Use

## Core Interpretation Rule
Mechanism claims should be framed as supported hypotheses unless strong longitudinal or interventional evidence exists.

## Literature Notes

### Brand et al. 2016
Integrating psychological and neurobiological considerations regarding the development and maintenance of specific Internet-use disorders: An Interaction of Person-Affect-Cognition-Execution (I-PACE) model. Neurosci Biobehav Rev. 2016;71:252-266. doi:10.1016/j.neubiorev.2016.08.033. PMID: 27590829.

Summary:
-   Proposes the I-PACE model for the development and maintenance of specific internet-use disorders.
-   Emphasizes interactions among predisposing traits, coping style, cue-reactivity, affective and cognitive responses, and reduced executive control.
-   Links theoretical mechanism to treatment implications while explicitly noting the need for more empirical testing.

Use in synthesis:
-   Use as the default framework when you need a mechanism model that extends beyond a single app or platform.

### Weinstein and Lejoyeux 2020
Neurobiological mechanisms underlying internet gaming disorder. Dialogues Clin Neurosci. 2020;22(2):113-126. doi:10.31887/DCNS.2020.22.2/aweinstein. PMID: 32699511.

Summary:
-   Reviews neurobiological correlates of IGD.
-   Useful for cue-reactivity, reward, and control-circuit discussions without implying definitive biomarkers.

Use in synthesis:
-   Use when the user asks for neurobiology, brain mechanisms, or similarities to other addictive disorders.

### León Méndez et al. 2024
Effects of internet and smartphone addiction on cognitive control in adolescents and young adults: A systematic review of fMRI studies. Neurosci Biobehav Rev. 2024;159:105572. doi:10.1016/j.neubiorev.2024.105572. PMID: 38320657.

Summary:
-   Included 21 fMRI studies.
-   Reported impairments in reward-related and executive-control regions, including ACC, insula, amygdala, DLPFC, and parietal or frontal areas.
-   Did not find clear differential cognitive-control effects separating internet addiction from smartphone addiction.

Use in synthesis:
-   Supports cautious claims that excessive digital-use syndromes involve reward and executive-control processes, but not highly specific neural signatures.

## AI-Chatbot and LLM Use: Reward Mechanisms and Cognitive Effects (2025–2026)

### Robayo-Pinzon et al. 2025
Robayo-Pinzon OJ, et al. Generative artificial intelligence use and dependence: an approach from behavioral economics. *[Journal TBC]*. 2025.

Summary:
-   Applied behavioral-economics methods to test the relative reinforcing value (RRV) of AI-chatbot use versus money.
-   Found that immediate access to chatbot use carries measurable reinforcing value for a portion of users.
-   Dependence scores differed significantly by daily AI-use time, supporting a dose-response pattern.

Use in synthesis:
-   Currently the strongest direct evidence for reward-based reinforcement in AI-chatbot use. The RRV framework is borrowed from substance-dependence and behavioral-addiction literatures; apply the same interpretive caution (reinforcing value ≠ addiction, but is a relevant antecedent variable). Use when the question is whether AI use has addiction-relevant reward properties.

### Shen et al. 2026
Shen X, et al. The AI Genie Phenomenon and Three Types of AI Chatbot Addiction. *CHI 2026 Conference Paper*. 2026.

Summary:
-   Argues that dependence across all three chatbot use types is driven by an "AI Genie" dynamic: systems deliver exactly what users want with minimal cognitive effort.
-   Distinguishes three dependence pathways: (1) escapist roleplay, (2) pseudosocial companion attachment, and (3) epistemic rabbit holes (compulsive information-seeking with minimal verification).
-   Effort-free fulfillment is proposed as the shared reinforcement mechanism.

Use in synthesis:
-   Use when linking AI-chatbot dependence to reward and effort-discounting mechanisms in I-PACE or dual-process frameworks. The taxonomy is conceptually useful but has not yet been tested psychometrically; treat as a theoretical framework pending empirical validation.

### Fang et al. 2025
Fang [first author], et al. How AI and Human Behaviors Shape Psychosocial Effects of Extended Chatbot Use. *[Preprint]*. 2025.

Summary:
-   In a four-week randomized study (N = 981), participants who voluntarily used the chatbot more showed worse outcomes across loneliness, real-world socialization, emotional dependence, and problematic AI use.
-   The harm pattern was driven by self-selected heavy use, not random assignment to high use — consistent with a role for pre-existing vulnerability or emergent reinforcement loop.

Use in synthesis:
-   The voluntary high-use mechanism finding is more relevant here than the overall RCT result. It supports the hypothesis that reward-sensitive individuals or those using chatbots for emotional regulation may be at elevated risk, paralleling cue-reactivity and coping-motivation models in general PIU.

### Catalan et al. 2026
Catalan [first author], et al. I'm Not Reading All of That. *[Journal/Conference TBC]*. 2026.

Summary:
-   Qualitative and observational study of software engineers using agentic coding assistants.
-   Found that cognitive engagement declined as work with agentic coding tools progressed.
-   Current systems provide weak support for reflection, verification, and meaning-making.
-   Relevant as evidence for cognitive offloading and reduced executive engagement in occupational AI use.

Use in synthesis:
-   Applicable when extending PIU/overreliance models to occupational AI use. The mechanism is cognitive disengagement rather than reward-based reinforcement; distinguish clearly from chatbot-companion dependence pathways. The paper does not test PIU as a clinical construct — it documents reduced reflection and verification behavior.

### Becker et al. / METR 2025
Becker [first author], et al. Measuring the Impact of Early-2025 AI on Experienced Open-Source Developer Productivity. *METR Technical Report*. 2025.

Summary:
-   Controlled study of experienced open-source developers using AI coding assistants.
-   Found a 19% slowdown in objective productivity despite participants' belief that AI had sped them up.
-   The subjective-objective mismatch is consistent with a positively biased subjective experience decoupled from objective performance.

Use in synthesis:
-   The mismatch is mechanistically relevant: a system that feels rewarding while objectively degrading performance mirrors the subjective-objective dissociation found in behavioral addictions. Use cautiously — this is an occupational productivity study, not a PIU or addiction study. Treat as adjacent evidence, not direct evidence for coding-agent addiction.

### Cui et al. 2025
Cui [first author], et al. The Effects of Generative AI on High-Skilled Work. *[Journal/Preprint TBC]*. 2025.

Summary:
-   Three field experiments with 4,867 developers; reported a 26.08% increase in completed tasks overall.
-   Gains were larger for less experienced developers.

Use in synthesis:
-   Necessary counterpoint to Becker et al. 2025. The divergence between the two studies may reflect differences in developer experience, task type, or measurement approach. Together they underscore that the occupational AI literature does not uniformly support either harm or benefit, and that overreliance and reward-mismatch findings should not be overgeneralized.
