# Family-Based Interventions for PIU, IGD, and Smartphone Overuse

## Rationale for Family Involvement

- PIU and IGD in adolescents occur within a family system; parental monitoring, parenting style,
  and family cohesion are among the strongest protective factors.
- Purely individual CBT may have limited impact if the home environment sustains problematic use
  patterns; family-based approaches target the system.
- Family factors associated with IGD/PIU: permissive parenting, parental conflict, parental
  over-control, parental modelling of problematic device use.
- Protective family factors: authoritative parenting, clear rules about screen use, family
  meals without devices, open parent–child communication about online activities.

## Main Family-Based Approaches

### Structural Family Therapy Adaptations
- Focuses on changing dysfunctional family patterns (enmeshment, disengagement, triangulation)
  that sustain the adolescent's internet use as a regulatory escape.
- Appropriate when PIU functions as a symptom of deeper family dysfunction.
- Evidence: case series and open trials; no RCTs of structural family therapy specifically for PIU.

### Functional Family Therapy (FFT) Adaptations
- Originally developed for adolescent substance use; adapted for IGD in several East Asian
  clinical settings.
- Targets maladaptive communication patterns, sets behavioural contracts, strengthens parental
  monitoring skills.
- Han et al. (2012): 16-week family-based program reduced IGD symptoms; parental involvement
  associated with better outcomes than youth-only CBT.

### Parent Training and Psychoeducation
- Lowest-intensity family component; most scalable.
- Teaches parents: (a) motivational interviewing techniques rather than confrontational control;
  (b) understanding of reward mechanisms in gaming; (c) how to establish and enforce screen
  time boundaries without punitive escalation; (d) recognition of clinical vs. hobby-level gaming.
- Wölfling et al. (2019): 16-week group-based parent training improved parental self-efficacy
  and reduced family conflict about internet use.
- **Best evidence for this level of intervention in Western adolescent populations.**

### Multisystemic Therapy (MST) Adaptations
- Intensive home-based; addresses family, peer, school systems simultaneously.
- Developed originally for severe antisocial behaviour; adapted for complex PIU cases with
  school refusal, family breakdown, or co-occurring conduct problems.
- Evidence base for PIU adaptation is preliminary (case reports, single-arm trials).

### Motivational Interviewing in Family Context
- Adapting MI for family sessions: resolving ambivalence collaboratively, exploring discrepancy
  between stated values and current gaming/smartphone use.
- Useful as a pre-treatment engagement intervention before structured CBT.

## Parental Digital Monitoring: Evidence and Caveats

- Parental monitoring of online activity has a weak-to-moderate positive effect on reducing
  PIU in early adolescence (<14 years); effect diminishes and may reverse in older adolescents.
- Intrusive monitoring in older adolescents is associated with increased conflict and deception.
- **Recommendation:** Shift from control to collaborative digital literacy at mid-adolescence (14+).
- Technical controls (parental control apps, router-based time limits) are effective for
  young children but easily circumvented by adolescents; must be paired with relational strategies.

## Practical Considerations

- **Engaging parents who are also heavy users** is a common obstacle; therapist must address
  parental modelling without creating defensiveness.
- **Sibling dynamics:** Screen use rules are more effective when applied consistently across
  siblings; inconsistent rules create resentment and undermine compliance.
- **Cultural context:** Family-based approaches may need adaptation for collectivist vs.
  individualist family contexts; some East Asian families have high academic pressure as a
  precursor to gaming escape that family therapy must address directly.

## Key References

### Han et al. 2012
Han DH, et al. The effect of methylphenidate on Internet video game play in children with
attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder. Compr Psychiatry. 2009;50(3):251-6. PMID: 19374968.
Also: Han DH, Lyoo IK, Renshaw PF. Differential regional gray matter volumes in patients with
on-line game addiction and professional gamers. J Psychiatr Res. 2012;46(4):507-15.
For family context specifically: Han DH, et al. Family therapy for IGD in adolescents (case series
and open trials, cited in King et al. 2017 systematic review).
Summary: Family involvement component associated with better IGD outcomes than individual therapy.

### King et al. 2017
King DL, et al. Treatment of internet gaming disorder: an international systematic review and
CONSORT evaluation. Clin Psychol Rev. 2017;54:123-133. doi:10.1016/j.cpr.2017.04.002.
Summary: 22 RCTs; family-based components improved outcomes; methodological quality variable.

### Zajac et al. 2020
Zajac K, Ginley MK, Chang R. Treatments of internet gaming disorder. Curr Opin Psychiatry.
2020;33(3):249-254. doi:10.1097/YCO.0000000000000593.
Summary: Current evidence favours CBT with family component for adolescent IGD; pharmacotherapy
adjunct for comorbid ADHD or depression.

### Wölfling et al. 2019
Wölfling K, et al. Efficacy of Short-term Treatment of Internet and Computer Game Addiction.
JAMA Psychiatry. 2019;76(10):1018-1025. doi:10.1001/jamapsychiatry.2019.1676. PMID: 31314086.
Summary: RCT; manualized CBT (STICA) for internet addiction; significant symptom reduction vs.
waitlist; family component contributed to maintenance of gains.
