# School-Based Interventions for PIU, IGD, and Smartphone Overuse

## Rationale for School-Based Delivery

- School settings reach high-risk adolescents before clinical severity develops; natural
  prevention platform.
- Teachers and school counsellors are positioned to identify early at-risk use patterns.
- Schools can implement universal (whole-class), selective (at-risk), and indicated
  (clinical referral) interventions in a stepped-care model.
- Most evidence comes from East Asian (South Korea, China, Taiwan) programs; generalisability
  to Western educational contexts requires caution.

## Universal Prevention Programs

### Digital Literacy Education
- Aims to build media literacy, critical consumption skills, and awareness of platform design
  mechanics (variable-ratio rewards, engagement traps).
- Systematic review (Throuvala et al. 2019): 11 programs reviewed; digital literacy components
  were the most consistently effective element.
- **Key content elements:** how notification systems work; passive vs. active use; social
  comparison effects; sleep hygiene around devices; modelling healthy use.
- Teacher delivery feasible; requires ~4–6 classroom sessions.

### Psychoeducation About PIU
- Structured curriculum explaining the difference between heavy/enthusiastic use and
  clinically impairing use; prevents pathologising normative digital engagement.
- Reduces stigma around seeking help for genuine PIU while correcting misconceptions
  about "screen time = harm."

### Korean National Prevention Program (Internet Addiction Prevention Center)
- Korea has the most developed national school PIU prevention infrastructure globally.
- Mandated screening (K-scale) for all 1st, 4th, 7th, 10th grade students.
- Tiered follow-up: low risk → brief psychoeducation; medium risk → group counselling;
  high risk → clinical referral.
- Outcome data show reduced PIU rates in screened vs. unscreened cohorts; programme
  design has been cited as a model by WHO.

## Selective Interventions (At-Risk Students)

### Group CBT Programs
- Small group format (6–12 students) targeting students who screen positive or are
  referred by teachers for PIU concerns.
- Typical components: cognitive restructuring of gaming beliefs, behavioural activation
  for non-digital activities, problem-solving social difficulties that trigger gaming/SMD,
  goal-setting and self-monitoring.
- Shek et al. (2013): 12-week group CBT in Chinese school; significant reduction in PIU
  scores vs. waitlist; sustained at 3-month follow-up.

### Mindfulness-Based Programs
- Adolescent-adapted mindfulness training (e.g., .b [dot-be] curriculum) targeting
  impulsivity and emotion dysregulation, which are risk factors for PIU.
- Limited PIU-specific evidence; promising for upstream risk-factor reduction.

### Peer-Support and Mentoring
- Older students trained as mentors for younger students showing PIU concerns.
- Low-cost; leverages normative peer influence pathways.
- Evidence is mostly qualitative and process-level; no RCT data.

## Indicated Interventions (High-Risk / Symptomatic)

### School-Based Counsellor CBT
- Individually delivered CBT by school counsellors trained in PIU protocols.
- Appropriate for students meeting IGD/PIU criteria who do not need specialist
  clinical referral.
- Referral pathways must be established before programme launch; students with
  psychiatric comorbidity (ADHD, depression) require specialist co-assessment.

### Motivational Interviewing in School Counselling
- MI techniques adapted for adolescent PIU; 2–4 sessions; focuses on building
  motivation for change rather than confrontational advice-giving.
- Young person-centred; particularly useful for adolescents with low baseline
  motivation to reduce use.

## Implementation Challenges

- **Teacher training burden:** Programmes requiring heavy teacher training show lower
  fidelity in implementation; prefer programmes with structured materials.
- **Normalisation risk:** Poorly designed psychoeducation may inadvertently normalise
  heavy use by framing it as "not really a disorder."
- **Screen time vs. content:** Programmes focusing on duration alone may miss the
  content/context dimension; PIU is not simply about hours.
- **Cultural fit:** East Asian programs may not translate directly; adjustment for
  Western academic pressure profiles, family structures, and digital cultures needed.

## Key References

### Throuvala et al. 2019
Throuvala MA, Griffiths MD, Rennoldson M, Kuss DJ. School-based prevention for adolescent
internet addiction: prevention is better than the cure. Addict Behav. 2019;89:24-32.
doi:10.1016/j.addbeh.2018.09.012. PMID: 30265872.
Summary: Systematic review of 11 prevention programs; digital literacy component most effective;
multicomponent programs outperform single-focus approaches.

### Shek et al. 2013
Shek DTL, Tang VMY, Lo CY. Evaluation of an internet addiction treatment program for Chinese
adolescents in Hong Kong. Adolescence. 2009;44(174):359-73. PMID: 19764279.
Summary: 12-week group CBT program in school setting; significant PIU reduction vs. waitlist.

### Chung et al. 2019
Chung S-J, et al. Nationwide survey on the prevalence and risk factors of gaming disorder in
Korean adolescents and psychoeducation programs. Asian J Psychiatr. 2019;46:55-60.
doi:10.1016/j.ajp.2019.09.030.
Summary: National data from Korean screening program; tiered intervention model described;
psychoeducation effective for low-risk tier.

### OECD 2019
OECD. Students, Computers and Learning: Making the Connection. Paris: OECD Publishing; 2015.
doi:10.1787/9789264239555-en.
Summary: Cross-national data on student technology use in schools; moderate use associated with
better academic outcomes; very high use associated with worse outcomes; school policies matter.
