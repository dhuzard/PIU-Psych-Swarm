# Diagnosis and Clinical Picture of Problematic Internet Use

## Key Clinical Points
-   Problematic internet use is an umbrella construct, not a single universally standardized diagnosis.
-   Internet gaming disorder has the clearest psychiatric framing in major nosologies, but broader PIU and internet addiction constructs remain heterogeneous.
-   Functional impairment, loss of control, salience, and persistence despite harm matter more than raw time online.

## Literature Notes

### Kuss et al. 2014
Internet addiction: a systematic review of epidemiological research for the last decade. Curr Pharm Des. 2014;20(25):4026-4052. doi:10.2174/13816128113199990617. PMID: 24001297.

Summary:
-   Reviewed 68 epidemiological studies.
-   Identified 21 different assessment instruments and no gold standard classification.
-   Reported prevalence varied widely, in part because tools and cut-offs differed.
-   PIU was associated with psychosocial difficulties and comorbid symptoms in adolescents and adults.

Use in synthesis:
-   Cite this when explaining why prevalence estimates and even the construct itself require careful qualification.

### Paulus et al. 2018
Internet gaming disorder in children and adolescents: a systematic review. Dev Med Child Neurol. 2018;60(7):645-659. doi:10.1111/dmcn.13754. PMID: 29633243.

Summary:
-   Reviews pediatric and adolescent IGD literature.
-   Supports the view that clinically relevant impairment exists in a minority of young people rather than in all frequent gamers.
-   Useful for separating gaming-specific disorder from broader PIU.

Use in synthesis:
-   Use when the task is child or adolescent psychiatry and the user needs a gaming-specific clinical picture.

### Kokka et al. 2021
Exploring the Effects of Problematic Internet Use on Adolescent Sleep: A Systematic Review. Int J Environ Res Public Health. 2021;18(2):760. doi:10.3390/ijerph18020760. PMID: 33477410.

Summary:
-   Across 12 studies, all included studies showed a negative correlation between adolescent sleep and PIU.
-   Reported links involved worse sleep quality, shorter sleep, and insomnia symptoms.
-   Family context, sex, and use motives moderated the association.

Use in synthesis:
-   Treat sleep disturbance as an important associated clinical domain, especially in adolescent assessments.

## AI-Chatbot and LLM Dependence: Clinical Picture (2025–2026)

### Yankouskaya, Liebherr, and Ali 2025
Yankouskaya A, Liebherr M, Ali S. Can ChatGPT Be Addictive? *[Viewpoint; journal TBC]*. 2025.

Summary:
-   Viewpoint paper (not a prevalence study); one of the clearest recent framings of the mechanism hypothesis.
-   Proposes that personalized responses, emotional validation, instant gratification, and continuous engagement may shift use from instrumental support toward dependence.
-   Does not establish clinical prevalence but articulates a theoretically coherent dependence pathway applicable to I-PACE framing.

Use in synthesis:
-   Use when explaining the plausible dependence mechanism for chatbot use; always flag it as a viewpoint, not an empirical finding.

### Ciudad-Fernández, von Hammerstein, and Billieux 2025
Ciudad-Fernández V, von Hammerstein C, Billieux J. People are not becoming "AIholic." *[Journal TBC]*. 2025.

Summary:
-   Main corrective paper in the 2025 AI-dependence debate.
-   Argues that evidence for "ChatGPT addiction" is still weak; multiple scales have been developed prematurely, and construct validity has not been robustly established.
-   Explicitly warns against pathologizing high engagement or ordinary reliance.

Use in synthesis:
-   Cite as the primary counterweight whenever AI-chatbot dependence claims risk overstating clinical certainty. It does not deny that dependence-adjacent patterns exist, only that the field has not demonstrated them rigorously enough to justify a disorder framing.

### Zhang et al. 2025 (mental health outcomes)
Zhang [first author], et al. Exploring AI chatbot usage behaviors and their association with mental health outcomes in Chinese university students. *[Journal TBC]*. 2025.

Summary:
-   Cross-sectional study; N = 1,004 Chinese university students.
-   About 45.8% reported recent AI-chatbot use.
-   Users showed higher depression scores than non-users.
-   Overuse or dependence was not the norm in the sample; association was observational and cross-sectional.

Use in synthesis:
-   Provides prevalence context (near-majority student use) and a mental-health signal, but the cross-sectional design prevents causal inference. Always state the sampling and design limitations.

### Perlis et al. 2026
Perlis RH, et al. Generative AI Use and Depressive Symptoms Among US Adults. *[Journal TBC]*. 2026.

Summary:
-   Large US survey; N = 20,847 adults.
-   Daily generative-AI use was associated with modestly higher depressive symptoms.
-   The association was clearest for personal use, not school or work use.

Use in synthesis:
-   One of the largest adult samples in this literature. The effect size is modest and the design is cross-sectional; causal attribution is not supported. Useful for scale and for pointing to personal-use context as a moderator.

### Nakagomi et al. 2026
Nakagomi A, et al. AI companions and subjective well-being. *[Journal TBC]*. 2026.

Summary:
-   Found companion-AI use associated with higher subjective well-being, especially among lonelier users and those with moderate friend-network size.
-   Does not cancel the risk literature but establishes that the field is not uniformly negative.

Use in synthesis:
-   Cite as a necessary counterpoint when synthesizing AI-dependence harm claims. The net effect likely depends on baseline social context and use purpose.

### Moore et al. 2026
Moore [first author], et al. Characterizing Delusional Spirals through Human-LLM Interaction. *[Journal/Preprint TBC]*. 2026.

Summary:
-   Relevant for high-risk, compulsive, or vulnerable use cases.
-   Documents patterns in which ongoing LLM interaction may amplify delusional cognition in susceptible individuals.

Use in synthesis:
-   Relevant to clinical risk stratification: standard PIU constructs may underestimate risk in users with pre-existing psychotic or delusional features. Apply with appropriate clinical caution.

### Zhu, Coifman, and Jin 2026
Zhu [first author], Coifman K, Jin Y. Understanding Risk and Dependency in AI Chatbot Use from User Discourse. *[Preprint/Journal TBC]*. 2026.

Summary:
-   Qualitative analysis of Reddit communities focused on AI-related harm and "chatbot addiction."
-   Extracted recurring experiential dimensions of dependency, self-regulation difficulty, and withdrawal-like discomfort from user narratives.

Use in synthesis:
-   Use for lived-experience framing and to ground clinical phenomenology in user-reported patterns. Reddit samples are not representative; treat as hypothesis-generating, not prevalence data.
