# Internet Gaming Disorder — Mechanisms and Neurocognition

## Theoretical Frameworks

### I-PACE Model (Brand et al. 2016, 2019)
The Interaction of Person-Affect-Cognition-Execution (I-PACE) model is the dominant
theoretical framework for IGD:

- **Person variables:** core psychiatric traits (ADHD symptoms, depression, social anxiety)
  interact with pre-existing reward sensitivity and impulsivity.
- **Affective and cognitive responses:** gaming cues trigger craving and attentional bias
  (cue-reactivity); explicit and implicit cognitions about gaming reinforce engagement.
- **Execution:** weakened inhibitory control and poor decision-making allow gaming to
  override competing activities.
- IGD develops when repeated gaming to regulate negative affect erodes self-regulatory capacity
  in a downward spiral.

*Note: I-PACE is a theoretical model, not an empirically validated causal chain.
Individual components have partial empirical support; the full model remains to be tested
in longitudinal designs.*

## Reward and Dopaminergic Mechanisms

- Gaming activates the mesolimbic dopamine system (ventral striatum, nucleus accumbens),
  producing reward signals comparable in neural signature to other reinforcing activities.
- Variable-ratio reward schedules (loot boxes, levelling mechanics) are particularly potent
  reinforcers by operant conditioning principles.
- **Tolerance:** neuroimaging studies suggest blunted striatal dopamine response to gaming cues
  in heavy gamers (analogous to substance tolerance), though replication is limited.
- **Cue reactivity:** IGD individuals show elevated craving responses and attentional bias
  to gaming-related cues in neuroimaging and dot-probe paradigms.

*Caution: Neuroimaging studies of IGD have very small samples (n < 30 in most fMRI studies),
limited replication, and significant methodological variation. Treat neuroimaging findings as
hypothesis-generating, not established mechanism.*

## Executive Function and Inhibitory Control

- Elevated impulsivity (motor and cognitive) in IGD samples across stop-signal, go/no-go,
  and delay-discounting tasks.
- Reduced response inhibition in IGD is among the most replicated cognitive findings
  (Weinstein et al. 2017 meta-analysis).
- Working memory deficits observed in some studies, particularly dual-task paradigms with
  gaming stimuli as distractors.
- **Causal ambiguity:** Pre-existing ADHD and impulsivity vulnerability may select individuals
  into IGD rather than IGD causing executive function deficits. Longitudinal data needed.

## Neuroimaging Findings (Summary)

- **Structural MRI:** Reduced grey matter in prefrontal and orbitofrontal cortex in some IGD
  cohorts; increased grey matter in striatum. Small samples; inconsistent across studies.
- **Functional MRI (resting state):** Altered functional connectivity in frontoparietal and
  default-mode networks; disrupted top-down regulatory circuits proposed.
- **fMRI (cue reactivity):** Elevated ventral striatum response to gaming cues in IGD vs.
  controls; linked to craving reports (Dong et al. 2017).
- **EEG:** Reduced P300 amplitude (attentional resource allocation marker) and elevated
  N200 (conflict detection) during inhibitory tasks; consistent with impaired executive control.

## Stress and Emotional Regulation

- IGD frequently develops in context of stress, social anxiety, and poor offline social competence.
- Gaming provides immediate negative-affect relief (avoidance/escape coping) that is negatively
  reinforced but does not resolve the underlying stressor.
- Allostatic load model: chronic stress increases vulnerability to reward-seeking behaviours
  including pathological gaming.

## Key References

### Brand et al. 2019
Brand M, Wegmann E, Stark R, et al. The Interaction of Person-Affect-Cognition-Execution
(I-PACE) model for addictive behaviors: Update, generalization to addictive behaviors beyond
internet-use disorders, and specification of the process character of addictive behaviors. Neurosci
Biobehav Rev. 2019;104:1-10. doi:10.1016/j.neubiorev.2019.06.032. PMID: 31271851.
Summary: Updated I-PACE theoretical model; extends to all internet-use disorders including
gaming, gambling, pornography viewing, and social networking.

### Weinstein et al. 2017
Weinstein A, Abu Rmeileh N, Brickman S. A meta-analytic review of the relationship between
internet gaming disorder and executive dysfunction. Neuropsychology. 2017;31(7):755-779.
doi:10.1037/neu0000367.
Summary: Meta-analysis of executive function in IGD; response inhibition deficit is the most
robust cognitive marker; small-to-medium effect sizes.

### Dong et al. 2017
Dong G, Potenza MN. Behavioural and brain responses related to Internet gaming and addiction.
In: Potenza MN, Faust M, Faust D (eds). The Oxford Handbook of Impulse Control Disorders.
Oxford: OUP; 2012. Chapter also: Dong G, et al. Cue-elicited craving in IGD: Neural substrates.
Addict Biol. 2017;22(3):641-651. doi:10.1111/adb.12347.
Summary: fMRI cue-reactivity; elevated ventral striatum BOLD signal in IGD players; correlated
with self-reported craving.

### Zajac et al. 2020
Zajac K, Ginley MK, Chang R. Treatments of internet gaming disorder. Curr Opin Psychiatry.
2020;33(3):249-254. doi:10.1097/YCO.0000000000000593. PMID: 32084073.
Summary: Review of CBT and pharmacotherapy; moderate efficacy for CBT targeting cognitive
distortions and executive control; pharmacotherapy data preliminary.
