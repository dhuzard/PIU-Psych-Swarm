# Telehealth and Digital Delivery Pathways for PIU and IGD

## Rationale for Telehealth Delivery

- Adolescents and young adults with PIU/IGD are typically the most digitally fluent age group;
  telehealth aligns with their preferred engagement channel.
- Access barriers: rural or remote populations, transport limitations, stigma around in-person
  mental health contact, waitlist pressures for face-to-face services.
- PIU patients in particular may have difficulty leaving the home (for the severe subset);
  telehealth reduces the functional barrier.
- Cost: digital delivery at scale can reduce per-head cost, though clinician supervision remains
  resource-intensive.

## Evidence Base for Telehealth PIU/IGD Treatment

### Synchronous Telehealth (Video-Based Therapy)

- Delivers CBT, motivational interviewing, or family-based protocols via secure video platform.
- Evidence for general mental health telehealth is strong (non-inferiority to in-person CBT
  for anxiety, depression); PIU/IGD-specific telehealth RCTs are emerging but limited.
- Key considerations: clinician training in video-adapted assessment; technology access equity;
  confidentiality (adolescents in household with gaming device and family present).

**Wölfling et al. (STICA protocol):** Originally designed for in-person delivery;
telehealth adaptation feasible; pilot data suggest comparable outcomes.

### Asynchronous / App-Based Self-Help

**Self-guided digital interventions (DIs) for PIU:**
- StayFocusd, Forest, Freedom, Screen Time (iOS) — app-based self-monitoring and blocking;
  evidence limited to usability studies; no RCT data for PIU populations.
- Cognitive training apps targeting impulsivity (e.g., CogniFit): preliminary evidence for
  executive function improvement; not yet tested in IGD-specific populations.

**Guided self-help programs:**
- Structured online programs with occasional therapist/coach contact (asynchronous messaging).
- Evidence from substance use disorders (guided self-help > pure self-help); extrapolation to
  PIU is plausible but not yet validated.

### Digital Therapeutics (DTx)

- Regulated software-based treatments (e.g., EndeavorRx model in ADHD); no FDA/CE-cleared
  DTx specifically for PIU/IGD as of 2025.
- WHO and APA have noted IGD as a priority area for DTx development.
- Ongoing clinical trials in East Asia testing gamified CBT platforms for IGD.

## Stepped-Care Framework for Telehealth

A practical stepped-care model for PIU/IGD:

| Step | Intensity | Format | Target |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Self-directed | App/website psychoeducation | At-risk (screen-positive, no impairment) |
| 2 | Guided self-help | Online program + asynchronous therapist messages | Mild IGD/PIU |
| 3 | Telehealth CBT | Weekly video sessions, 8–16 sessions | Moderate IGD/PIU |
| 4 | Intensive telehealth + family | Video CBT + parent sessions | Severe, with family conflict |
| 5 | Inpatient / intensive outpatient | Residential or day program | Severe + complex comorbidity |

Step-up criteria: lack of progress after ≥8 sessions; active suicidality; substance use
comorbidity; severe self-neglect; family safeguarding concerns.

## Barriers and Equity Considerations

- **Digital divide:** Telehealth benefits primarily those with reliable broadband and private
  space; rural and low-SES populations have poorer access.
- **Device paradox:** Delivering PIU treatment over the same device used problematically
  requires explicit protocol design (e.g., computer-based therapy session with phone
  in another room; router-level session scheduling).
- **Engagement drop-off:** Digital self-help programs show high attrition (30–60% dropout
  by session 4 in most studies); active outreach (brief therapist check-in calls) substantially
  improves completion.
- **Stigma:** Young people may prefer digital format precisely because of stigma; this is an
  asset for engagement but means severity may be underestimated.

## Rural and Low-Access Populations

- Telehealth has particular utility for PIU in rural and remote communities where
  specialist care is unavailable and internet access is the primary entertainment channel.
- Text/SMS-based brief interventions (5–10 messages per week) show moderate engagement
  in substance use; PIU-adapted SMS programs are under development.
- Peer-led online support groups (moderated forums, Discord servers) provide low-cost
  supplementary support but require clinical oversight to prevent unhelpful peer reinforcement.

## Key References

### Wölfling et al. 2019
Wölfling K, et al. Efficacy of Short-term Treatment of Internet and Computer Game Addiction:
A Randomized Clinical Trial. JAMA Psychiatry. 2019;76(10):1018-1025.
doi:10.1001/jamapsychiatry.2019.1676. PMID: 31314086.
Summary: RCT of in-person STICA protocol; 90% completion; strong efficacy. Foundational for
telehealth adaptation work.

### Barak et al. 2008
Barak A, Hen L, Boniel-Nissim M, Shapira N. A comprehensive review and a meta-analysis of the
effectiveness of internet-based psychotherapeutic interventions. J Technol Hum Serv.
2008;26(2-4):109-160. doi:10.1080/15228830802094429.
Summary: Meta-analysis; internet-based CBT broadly effective across conditions; guided > unguided
formats; effect sizes comparable to face-to-face for mild-to-moderate presentations.

### Linardon & Fuller-Tyszkiewicz 2020
Linardon J, Fuller-Tyszkiewicz M. Attrition and adherence in smartphone-delivered interventions
for mental health problems: A systematic review and meta-analysis. J Consult Clin Psychol.
2020;88(1):1-13. doi:10.1037/ccp0000459. PMID: 31916796.
Summary: App-based mental health interventions; average dropout ~43%; active human contact
element most protective against dropout.

### Starcevic & Aboujaoude 2017
Starcevic V, Aboujaoude E. Internet addiction: reappraisal of an increasingly inadequate concept.
CNS Spectr. 2017;22(1):7-13. doi:10.1017/S1092852915000863. PMID: 26831456.
Summary: Critique of internet addiction framing; recommends specific-disorder approach
(IGD, SMD) for treatment planning; directly relevant to stepped-care protocol design.
