# Prevalence, Risk, and Measurement in Problematic Internet Use

## Working Cautions
-   Prevalence estimates are highly sensitive to screening tool choice and cut-off decisions.
-   Youth samples and convenience samples often produce higher reported rates.
-   Protective factors such as resilience are relevant, but measurement heterogeneity remains substantial.

## Literature Notes

### Kuss et al. 2014
Internet addiction: a systematic review of epidemiological research for the last decade. Curr Pharm Des. 2014;20(25):4026-4052. doi:10.2174/13816128113199990617. PMID: 24001297.

Summary:
-   Found prevalence estimates ranging from 0.8% to 26.7% across reviewed studies.
-   The spread reflects inconsistent instruments and cut-offs more than a stable global base rate.

### Stevens et al. 2021
Global prevalence of gaming disorder: A systematic review and meta-analysis. Aust N Z J Psychiatry. 2021;55(6):553-568. doi:10.1177/0004867420962851. PMID: 33028074.

Summary:
-   Meta-analyzed 53 studies with 226,247 participants from 17 countries.
-   Reported a pooled gaming-disorder prevalence of 3.05%, with a more conservative estimate of 1.96% under stricter sampling criteria.
-   Screening-tool choice explained most heterogeneity.
-   Higher estimates were linked to adolescent samples, lower cut-offs, and smaller samples.

Use in synthesis:
-   Use this when someone asks for prevalence, but always mention the heterogeneity and sampling caveats.

### Hidalgo-Fuentes et al. 2023
Problematic Internet Use and Resilience: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis. Nurs Rep. 2023;13(1):337-350. doi:10.3390/nursrep13010032. PMID: 36976684.

Summary:
-   Reviews resilience as a protective factor in PIU.
-   Supports including protective resources, not just risk factors, in psychiatric and public-health framing.

Use in synthesis:
-   Useful when the task involves prevention, school mental health, or resilience-informed interventions.

### Kokka et al. 2021
Exploring the Effects of Problematic Internet Use on Adolescent Sleep: A Systematic Review. Int J Environ Res Public Health. 2021;18(2):760. doi:10.3390/ijerph18020760. PMID: 33477410.

Summary:
-   Sleep impairment is a recurrent correlate in adolescent PIU literature.
-   Supports treating sleep as both an associated harm and a clinically relevant assessment target.

## AI-Chatbot and LLM Dependence: Measurement and Prevalence (2025–2026)

### Chen et al. 2025
Chen [first author], et al. Development and validation of the conversational AI dependence scale. *Frontiers [in Psychology/Psychiatry]*. 2025.

Summary:
-   Developed and validated a 20-item Conversational AI Dependence (CAI) scale in Chinese college samples.
-   Found that CAI dependence positively predicted sleep problems, functional difficulties, depression, anxiety, and stress, while negatively predicting subjective well-being.
-   Scale construction explicitly draws from PIU and behavioral addiction literatures.

Use in synthesis:
-   Currently the most comprehensive psychometric development paper in this domain. Note that it is based on Chinese college samples (convenience, single culture) and requires independent cross-cultural replication. Do not treat it as a clinical diagnostic instrument.

### Yankouskaya et al. 2025 (LLM-D12)
Yankouskaya A, et al. LLM-D12: A dependency scale for large language model use. *[Preprint/Journal TBC]*. 2025.

Summary:
-   Developed the LLM-D12 scale with two conceptually distinct dimensions: Instrumental Dependency (ordinary cognitive offloading and task reliance) and Relationship Dependency (companion-like or socially meaningful attachment).
-   The two-factor structure is useful because it separates functionally benign from clinically concerning use patterns.

Use in synthesis:
-   Use when precision about the type of dependence matters. The Instrumental/Relationship distinction maps roughly onto the task-use vs. companion-use distinction found elsewhere in the literature. Replication in non-student samples is still needed.

### Zhang et al. 2025 (AI chatbot dependence and internet/smartphone overlap)
Zhang [first author], et al. Investigating AI Chatbot Dependence. *[Journal TBC]*. 2025.

Summary:
-   Explicitly links AI-chatbot dependence with internet dependence and smartphone dependence.
-   Reports a moderate positive correlation between AI-chatbot dependence and both internet and smartphone dependence measures.
-   Mental-health associations were weaker than those for internet/smartphone dependence.
-   Usage purpose moderated the association.

Use in synthesis:
-   Key paper for establishing construct overlap between AI-chatbot dependence and existing PIU/smartphone-dependence measures. The moderate (not high) correlation suggests meaningful shared variance without full construct equivalence. Cite when discussing whether AI-dependence scales add unique information beyond existing PIU instruments.

### Fang et al. 2025
Fang [first author], et al. How AI and Human Behaviors Shape Psychosocial Effects of Extended Chatbot Use. *[Preprint]*. 2025.

Summary:
-   Four-week randomized study; N = 981 participants; over 300,000 messages analyzed.
-   Key finding: participants who voluntarily used the chatbot more (not the randomly assigned high-use group) showed worse outcomes across loneliness, real-world socialization, emotional dependence, and problematic AI use.
-   The voluntary high-use pattern may reflect a self-selection mechanism consistent with PIU risk models.

Use in synthesis:
-   Currently one of the strongest empirical studies in this literature given the longitudinal design and scale. The voluntary vs. assigned use distinction is methodologically important: the randomized arm alone did not produce harm, but the self-selected high-use pattern did. Treat as preprint until peer-reviewed.

### Noshin and Sultana 2026
Noshin N, Sultana A. The Illusion of Agreement with ChatGPT. *[Preprint/Journal TBC]*. 2026.

Summary:
-   User-discourse analysis; identifies user-reported harms including delusion, unsupervised psychological support, and addiction-like concerns.
-   Complements Zhu et al. 2026 in documenting self-reported dependence experiences.

Use in synthesis:
-   Qualitative and user-discourse evidence; treat as hypothesis-generating for scale development or clinical phenomenology, not as prevalence data.
