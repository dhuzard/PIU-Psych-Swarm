# Smartphone Overuse / Problematic Smartphone Use — Epidemiology and Measurement

## Measurement Landscape

PSU prevalence is arguably the most instrument-sensitive of all PIU sub-constructs.
Key issues:

- **Scale proliferation:** SAS, SAS-SV, MPPUS, PMPUQ, SPAI, and >50 other instruments.
  A 2019 systematic review (Rozgonjuk et al.) identified 41 different PSU scales in the literature.
- **Cut-off arbitrariness:** Most cut-offs derive from percentile splits (top 10%, top quartile)
  or item-mean thresholds — not clinical validation. Different cut-offs on the same scale
  produce 2–4× differences in reported prevalence.
- **Duration vs. dysfunction:** Many scales conflate daily duration of use with functional
  impairment. Duration alone is a weak proxy for disorder.
- **Platform shift:** Instruments validated on feature-phone or early-smartphone populations
  (pre-2015) may not capture current app-specific compulsive behaviours (TikTok, social feeds).

**Rule:** Always name the scale, version, cut-off, and sampling frame. Prevalence figures
without this context are not interpretable.

## Key Prevalence Estimates

### Adolescent samples

**Khoury et al. 2021 (meta-analysis)**
- Pooled prevalence: **26.8%** (95% CI 22.2–31.8%) using SAS-SV.
- Females: 29.0%; Males: 21.5%.
- High heterogeneity (I² = 98.7%) — pooled figure should be treated as approximate.

**Busch & McCarthy 2021 (meta-analysis, adolescents)**
- Longitudinal studies (n=7): PSU associated with depression (r=0.20) and anxiety (r=0.18) over time.
- Causal inference limited; confounding by pre-existing mental health is likely.

### University students

**Rozgonjuk et al. 2020 (Estonian university sample)**
- SAS-SV ≥32; prevalence 29% meeting cut-off in university students.
- PSU associated with exam anxiety and procrastination.

**Lopez-Fernandez et al. 2017 (11-country study)**
- SAS-SV; university student samples across Europe and Asia.
- Prevalence range: 7% (Germany) to 40% (China) — extreme cross-national range suggests
  cut-off and cultural response-style effects rather than true disorder difference.

### General population

**Systematic reviews note a dearth of population-representative surveys.** Most PSU
research uses convenience, student, or school samples. Population-representative
estimates in the 8–15% range are plausible given that student samples may
overestimate by 1.5–2×.

## Demographic Patterns

- **Sex:** Females > males on most PSU scales; driven by social networking and communication use.
- **Age:** Young adults (18–25) show highest rates; rates decline steadily after 30.
- **Socioeconomic status:** Limited data; smartphone penetration is near-universal in
  high-income countries, reducing access-related confounding.
- **Cultural factors:** East Asian university samples show higher PSU scores; some evidence
  this reflects response-style differences (acquiescence bias) rather than true disorder rates.

## Key References

### Khoury et al. 2021
Khoury JM, et al. Prevalence of problematic smartphone use: meta-analysis. J Addict Dis.
2021;39(3):296-307. doi:10.1080/10550887.2021.1894474.
Summary: Best available meta-analysis for SAS-SV based studies; pooled 26.8% but extreme I².

### Lopez-Fernandez et al. 2017
Lopez-Fernandez O, et al. Problematic smartphone use in an international sample across 11 countries.
Comput Human Behav. 2017;75:210-220. doi:10.1016/j.chb.2017.05.004.
Summary: Cross-national study; 7%–40% range illustrates the scale/culture confound problem.

### Busch & McCarthy 2021
Busch PA, McCarthy S. Reframing screen time for youth: Systematic review. Comput Human Behav.
2021;116:106636. doi:10.1016/j.chb.2020.106636.
Summary: Longitudinal meta-analysis; small-to-moderate effects of PSU on depression/anxiety;
cautions against causal inference from cross-sectional data.

### Rozgonjuk et al. 2020
Rozgonjuk D, Kattago M, Täht K. Social media use in lectures mediates the relationship between
procrastination and problematic smartphone use. Comput Human Behav. 2020;103:197-204.
doi:10.1016/j.chb.2019.09.011.
Summary: University sample; procrastination–PSU link; SAS-SV validation in Estonian population.
