# Internet Gaming Disorder — Epidemiology and Measurement

## Measurement Heterogeneity (Critical Context)

IGD prevalence estimates are unreliable as cross-study comparisons because:

1. **Instrument variety:** IGDS9-SF, GASA, GAS, IGD-20, KS-20, PVP-Q, Lemmens 7-item, and others —
   all measuring partially overlapping but non-identical constructs.
2. **Cut-off variation:** DSM-5 Section III proposed ≥5/9 criteria but this is unvalidated.
   Studies apply ≥4, ≥5, or dimensional scores with different thresholds.
3. **Time-frame differences:** past-year, past-month, and current criteria give incomparable figures.
4. **Sampling frames:** clinical samples, school samples, online gamer communities, and
   population-representative surveys differ radically in base rates.

**Rule:** When citing any IGD prevalence figure, state: (a) instrument name, (b) cut-off applied,
(c) sampling frame, (d) country and age group.

## Key Epidemiological Findings

### Global meta-analyses

**Stevens et al. 2021 (Psychological Medicine)**
- 53 studies; n > 150,000 participants.
- Pooled prevalence: **3.05%** (95% CI 2.38–3.71%) in general population samples.
- Higher in East Asia (3.9%) vs. Europe/North America (2.3%): likely reflects sampling bias and
  cultural gaming norms, not necessarily true disorder-rate difference.
- Risk factors: male sex, younger age (adolescents > adults), ADHD symptoms.

**Mihara & Higuchi 2017 (Psychiatry and Clinical Neurosciences)**
- 190 studies reviewed; prevalence ranged 0.7%–27.5% due to instrument heterogeneity.
- Recommended standardisation of DSM-5 criteria before further prevalence work.

### Population-representative surveys

**Mößle & Rehbein 2013 (German national sample)**
- Representative sample of German adolescents; IGD 3.7% (males) / 0.3% (females) using
  a 14-item scale.
- Strong male predominance consistent across Western studies.

**Gentile et al. 2017 (Singapore longitudinal)**
- 3,034 children followed 2-year cohort; ~9% pathological gaming at baseline;
  ~84% desisted over 2 years, suggesting many screen-positive children are transient users
  rather than persistent disorder cases.

## Sampling Frame and Bias Notes

- **Online/gamer community recruitment:** inflates prevalence; should not be extrapolated to
  general population.
- **School samples in East Asia:** often overrepresent heavy gamers due to classroom location
  of gaming culture; interpret East Asian figures in this context.
- **Self-report bias:** adolescents may under-report or over-report depending on social norms.
- **Response bias in clinical samples:** clinical samples have 5–10× higher rates than community
  samples by design; never apply clinical prevalence to population burden estimates.

## Demographic Patterns

- **Sex:** Male predominance is robust across cultures (M:F ratio approximately 3:1 in
  population samples). Note: female IGD may be underdetected if instruments were validated
  on male gaming populations.
- **Age:** Peak in adolescence (12–18) and young adults (18–25). Rates decline with age;
  adult rates rarely reported above 2% in representative samples.
- **Gaming platform:** PC/online gaming associated with higher IGD rates than console gaming;
  mobile gaming now emerging as a separate exposure category needing re-norming.
- **Socioeconomic factors:** Limited data; some evidence that lower-SES adolescents use gaming
  as a low-cost leisure activity, possibly elevating exposure.

## Longitudinal and Persistence Data

- **Gentile et al. 2017:** 84% of screen-positive adolescents remitted over 2 years without
  intervention — persistence is not the norm.
- **Brunborg et al. 2014:** 1-year follow-up; remission common; problem gaming appears more
  episodic than chronic for most individuals.
- **Implication:** Cross-sectional prevalence estimates overstate the burden of persistent IGD;
  caution required when extrapolating to lifetime disorder prevalence.

## Key References

### Stevens et al. 2021
Stevens MWR, et al. Global prevalence of gaming disorder. Aust N Z J Psychiatry.
2021;55(6):553-568. doi:10.1177/0004867420962851. PMID: 33040631.
Summary: Best available meta-analysis; pooled prevalence 3.05%; cross-regional heterogeneity.

### Mihara & Higuchi 2017
Mihara S, Higuchi S. Cross-sectional and longitudinal epidemiological studies of Internet gaming
disorder: A systematic review of the literature. Psychiatry Clin Neurosci. 2017;71(7):425-444.
doi:10.1111/pcn.12532. PMID: 28436212.
Summary: 190 studies reviewed; profound heterogeneity; standardisation of criteria needed.

### Gentile et al. 2017
Gentile DA, et al. Internet Gaming Disorder in Children and Adolescents. Pediatrics.
2017;140(Suppl 2):S81-S85. doi:10.1542/peds.2016-1758H. PMID: 29093387.
Summary: Longitudinal data; majority of pathological gamers at baseline had remitted by 2-year
follow-up; persistence is the minority outcome.
