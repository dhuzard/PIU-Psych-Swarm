# Clinical Pathology Interpretation in Preclinical Toxicology

## Species-Specific Caveats

### Rat
-   ALP: multiple isoforms; bone ALP elevations common in young growing rats and do not indicate hepatic injury without corroborating histopathology.
-   ALT: highly liver-specific in rodents; elevation >3× concurrent control warrants histopathological correlation.
-   GGT: low baseline in rat liver; elevation can indicate biliary involvement.
-   Cholesterol: low baseline in rats; diet and compound effects can cause marked changes.
-   RBC/Hgb: susceptible to stress erythropoiesis artifacts in poor blood collection technique.

### Mouse
-   Very small blood volumes limit clinical pathology panel breadth.
-   ALT baseline is higher and more variable than rat.
-   Platelet counts can be artificially low due to platelet clumping in EDTA tubes.

### Dog (Beagle)
-   ALP: multiple isoforms including corticosteroid-induced (CALP); CALP elevation does not indicate hepatic injury.
-   ALT: less specific for liver in dogs than in rodents.
-   Cholesterol: baseline higher than rodents; more sensitive marker of hypothyroidism.
-   Neutrophils: stress leukogram (neutrophilia, lymphopenia) common with handling.

### Non-Human Primate
-   High interindividual variability; historical control database essential for interpretation.
-   CK elevation can result from intramuscular injection; must be distinguished from myotoxicity.

## Key Reference Ranges
Reference ranges should always be confirmed against:
1. Concurrent control data from the same study (primary reference).
2. Historical control data from the same facility, strain, sex, and age range (secondary reference).
3. Published reference intervals (tertiary, for directional orientation only).

Key published reference: Matsuzawa T, et al. Standard Hematological and Serum Biochemical Values in Different Strains of Rats. Lab Anim. [Confirm citation via LitScout.]
