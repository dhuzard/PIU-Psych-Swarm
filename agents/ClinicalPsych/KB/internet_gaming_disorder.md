# Internet Gaming Disorder (IGD) — Clinical Picture

## Nosological Status

Internet gaming disorder (IGD) occupies a formal but contested position in current nosologies.

- **ICD-11 (WHO, 2022):** "Gaming Disorder" (6C51) is a fully recognised condition requiring at
  least 12 months of impaired control, prioritisation of gaming over daily activities, and
  continuation or escalation despite negative consequences. Both online and offline gaming are covered.
- **DSM-5 / DSM-5-TR (APA):** "Internet Gaming Disorder" appears in Section III (Emerging Measures
  and Models), classified as a condition requiring further study rather than a diagnosable disorder.
  Nine proposed criteria parallel substance-use disorder criteria.
- **Critical note:** When discussing IGD, always state whether the source uses ICD-11 or DSM-5
  framing; conflation of these produces incomparable prevalence estimates.

## Core Clinical Criteria (DSM-5 Section III — Nine Criteria)

1. Preoccupation with gaming
2. Withdrawal symptoms when gaming is not available (irritability, anxiety, sadness)
3. Tolerance: need for increasing amounts of gaming
4. Unsuccessful attempts to control gaming
5. Loss of interest in other activities or hobbies
6. Continued excessive gaming despite awareness of problems
7. Deception of family members or therapists about gaming
8. Use of gaming to escape or relieve negative moods
9. Jeopardising or losing a significant relationship, job, or educational opportunity

A DSM-5 research threshold of ≥5 of 9 criteria over 12 months has been widely used but never
formally validated. Studies using different cut-offs (≥4, ≥5) produce non-comparable prevalence figures.

## Prevalence Estimates

- Global estimates range from ~1% to 5% of gamers, varying by instrument and population.
- Higher rates (up to 10–15%) reported in East Asian adolescent male samples, likely reflecting
  sampling bias toward internet café and high-use populations.
- Lemmens et al. (2015): prevalence 1.4% in Dutch adolescents using a 7-item IGD scale.
- Stevens et al. (2021, *Psychological Medicine* meta-analysis): weighted prevalence 3.05%
  (95% CI 2.38–3.71%) in general population samples; substantially higher in clinical and
  self-selected online samples — always note sampling frame when citing.

## Comorbidity Profile

- ADHD: Consistently elevated ADHD symptoms in IGD samples; shared impulsivity and reward-sensitivity
  pathway proposed (Gentile et al. 2012; Mihara & Higuchi 2017).
- Depression and anxiety: Strong and replicable associations; causal direction unresolved.
- Autism spectrum: Elevated IGD rates in ASD populations; may reflect social refuge-seeking.
- Avoidant coping: IGD often functions as escape from offline stressors; distinguish primary disorder
  from secondary coping response before diagnosing.

## Diagnostic Cautions

- Functional impairment is mandatory for any diagnosis — not time spent gaming.
- Adolescent males who play 4–6 hours daily without impairment do NOT meet criteria.
- Misclassifying passionate gamers as disordered pathologises ordinary hobby engagement.
- IGD is not equivalent to problematic internet use (PIU): a gaming-specific construct should not be
  generalised to all internet activities.

## Key References

### Petry et al. 2014
Petry NM, et al. An international consensus for assessing internet gaming disorder using the new
DSM-5 approach. Addiction. 2014;109(9):1399-406. doi:10.1111/add.12457. PMID: 24456155.
Summary: Expert consensus paper proposing nine DSM-5 criteria; foundational for IGD field.

### Lemmens et al. 2015
Lemmens JS, Valkenburg PM, Gentile DA. The Internet Gaming Disorder Scale. Psychol Assess.
2015;27(2):567-82. doi:10.1037/pas0000062. PMID: 25558970.
Summary: Validated 9-item and 27-item IGD scales; demonstrated ~1.4% prevalence in Dutch adolescents.

### Stevens et al. 2021
Stevens MW, et al. Global prevalence of gaming disorder: A systematic review and meta-analysis.
Aust N Z J Psychiatry. 2021;55(6):553-568. doi:10.1177/0004867420962851. PMID: 33040631.
Summary: 53 studies; pooled prevalence 3.05%; prevalence inflated in self-selected and clinical samples.

### King et al. 2020
King DL, et al. Treatment of Internet Gaming Disorder: An international systematic review and CONSORT
evaluation. Clin Psychol Rev. 2017;54:123-133. doi:10.1016/j.cpr.2017.04.002. PMID: 28458094.
Summary: 22 RCTs reviewed; CBT and family-based approaches show moderate efficacy; quality variable.
