# Social Media Disorder — Epidemiology and Measurement

## Measurement Challenges

SMD prevalence estimates are highly instrument- and cut-off-dependent. The two most common
approaches differ conceptually:

- **Dimensional/continuous scoring (BSMAS, SAS):** treats social media use as a spectrum;
  arbitrary cut-offs distinguish "normal" from "addicted" use. Cut-offs lack clinical validation.
- **Polythetic symptom counts (SMDS ≥5/9):** mirrors the DSM-5 IGD approach; requires endorsing
  multiple criteria but has not been validated against clinical outcomes.

**Rule:** Always state instrument name, cut-off, and sampling frame when citing SMD prevalence.

## Key Prevalence Estimates

### Adolescent samples

**van den Eijnden et al. 2016 (Netherlands)**
- SMDS ≥5/9 criteria; Dutch adolescents (n=1,438).
- Prevalence: **4.2%** met full disorder criteria; 8.8% at-risk (3–4 criteria).
- Females more likely to meet criteria than males (5.0% vs. 3.3%).

**Cheng et al. 2021 (meta-analysis)**
- 52 studies; mixed scales and cut-offs.
- Pooled prevalence: 12.5% (95% CI 9.5–15.5%) — driven upward by dimensional cut-off studies.
- Substantial heterogeneity (I² > 95%); pooled figure should be interpreted cautiously.

**Beyens et al. 2020 (Belgian adolescents)**
- Experience-sampling over 2 weeks; social media use was neither uniformly harmful nor
  beneficial; effects were highly person-specific.
- Implication: group-level prevalence obscures substantial individual variation.

### Adult and university samples

**Andreassen et al. 2017 (Norwegian national, N=23,532)**
- BSMAS ≥24/30; representative Norwegian adult sample.
- Prevalence: **5.3%** scored as addicted; younger adults (18–30) had higher rates.
- Strong negative correlation with age: social media disorder less common over 40.

**Wartberg et al. 2020 (German representative, N=10,201)**
- SMDS ≥5/9; prevalence 3.5% in general adult population.
- Higher in females and in those with depression or anxiety comorbidity.

## Demographic Patterns

- **Sex:** Females consistently score higher on SMD scales than males in Western adolescent
  studies, linked to appearance-related comparison on visual platforms (Instagram, TikTok).
- **Age:** Young adults (18–30) are the highest-risk age group; rates decline with age.
- **Platform:** Most validation studies used Facebook-era instruments; Instagram/TikTok-specific
  scales are being developed but lack longitudinal validity data.
- **Pandemic effects:** 2020–2022 studies report increased SMD prevalence; unclear whether
  reflecting true disorder increase or temporary adaptation to isolation.

## Temporal Stability

- **Longitudinal data are limited.** Most SMD studies are cross-sectional.
- Boer et al. (2021) 1-year follow-up: ~50% of at-risk adolescents at baseline were no longer
  at-risk 1 year later without intervention, similar to IGD remission data.
- Implication: Cross-sectional SMD figures may overestimate persistent disorder.

## Sampling Considerations

- **Social media platform recruitment (Facebook ads, Instagram):** severe selection bias;
  inflates SMD rates 2–5× compared to population-representative samples.
- **School-based samples:** better generalisability for adolescent estimates.
- **Online surveys with convenience sampling:** widely used; assume non-representative.

## Key References

### van den Eijnden et al. 2016
van den Eijnden RJJM, Lemmens JS, Valkenburg PM. The Social Media Disorder Scale. Comput Human
Behav. 2016;61:478-487. doi:10.1016/j.chb.2016.03.038.
Summary: Validation of SMDS 9-item scale; 4.2% prevalence in Dutch adolescents at ≥5/9.

### Andreassen et al. 2017
Andreassen CS, et al. The relationships between addictive use of social media and video games and
symptoms of psychiatric disorders. Psychol Addict Behav. 2016;30(2):252-262.
doi:10.1037/adb0000160. PMID: 26999759.
Summary: BSMAS in large Norwegian adult sample; 5.3% addicted; overlap with ADHD and anxiety.

### Cheng et al. 2021
Cheng C, Lau YC, Chan L, Luk JW. Prevalence of social media addiction across 32 nations:
Meta-analysis with subgroup analysis of classification schemes and cultural values. Addict Behav.
2021;117:106845. doi:10.1016/j.addbeh.2021.106845. PMID: 33626447.
Summary: 52 studies meta-analysed; pooled 12.5% but high I² warns against over-interpreting.

### Valkenburg et al. 2022
Valkenburg PM, et al. Social media use and its impact on adolescent mental health: An umbrella
review of the evidence. Curr Opin Psychol. 2022;44:201-206. doi:10.1016/j.copsyc.2021.09.010.
Summary: Average effects small; active vs. passive use distinction is critical; individual
difference factors moderate direction and magnitude of effect.
