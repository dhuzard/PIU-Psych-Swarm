# Social Media Disorder (SMD) — Clinical Picture

## Nosological Status

Social media disorder (SMD) is NOT a recognised diagnosis in ICD-11 or DSM-5. It is an
active research construct describing a pattern of problematic social media use characterised by
loss of control, functional impairment, and mood-driven engagement. Key distinctions:

- **Not equivalent to heavy social media use.** Frequency of use alone, even several hours daily,
  does not constitute disorder. Impairment of daily functioning is the threshold criterion.
- **Distinct from internet addiction.** SMD targets a specific platform-type rather than
  generalised internet use; some individuals have SMD without other PIU features.
- **Distinct from FOMO (fear of missing out).** FOMO is a dimensional risk factor, not a disorder.

## Assessment Instruments

- **Social Media Disorder Scale (SMDS):** van den Eijnden et al. 2016; 9 items mapping the
  nine DSM-5 IGD criteria onto social media use; cut-off ≥5/9 criteria used in most studies.
- **Bergen Social Media Addiction Scale (BSMAS):** Andreassen et al. 2016; 6-item scale modelled
  on the Bergen Facebook Addiction Scale; uses 1–5 Likert scoring; suggested cut-off ≥24/30.
- **Problematic Social Media Use (PSMU) scale:** various versions; less standardised than BSMAS.
- **Key caveat:** Different instruments produce incomparable prevalence estimates; always name
  the instrument and cut-off when citing a figure.

## Prevalence Estimates

- Andreassen et al. (2016): ~5.3% of Norwegian adults met BSMAS cut-off for social media addiction.
- Cheng et al. (2021, meta-analysis): Pooled prevalence ~6–13% across adolescent samples;
  wide CI reflecting instrument heterogeneity.
- Adolescent females consistently show higher SMD rates than males across Western samples;
  pattern reverses in some East Asian samples.
- Pandemic-era studies (2020-2022) report elevated rates; temporal stability unclear.

## Clinical Features

- Mood modification: using social media to escape negative affect (anxiety, boredom, loneliness).
- Salience: preoccupation with social media even when engaged in other activities.
- Conflict: social media use causing interpersonal conflict or interfering with sleep and study.
- Withdrawal: irritability, restlessness when access is restricted.
- Relapse: repeated failed attempts to cut back.

## Comorbidity Profile

- Depression and anxiety: consistently associated; bidirectional relationship proposed.
  Negative social comparison mechanisms implicated in body image disturbance (adolescent females).
- Sleep disturbance: screen use at bedtime and blue-light disruption of circadian rhythms.
- Loneliness: cross-sectional correlation robust; heavy users may use platforms to manage loneliness
  but evidence that it worsens loneliness through passive consumption.
- ADHD: impulsivity and novelty-seeking may increase vulnerability to SMD.

## Diagnostic Cautions

- Do not diagnose SMD on frequency alone. Heavy daily use with no impairment ≠ disorder.
- Social media use may serve positive social functions in isolated or marginalised populations;
  net harm must be demonstrated, not assumed.
- SMD construct stability across platforms (Instagram, TikTok, X) is unverified; most validation
  studies used Facebook/Instagram cohorts.

## Key References

### van den Eijnden et al. 2016
van den Eijnden RJJM, Lemmens JS, Valkenburg PM. The Social Media Disorder Scale. Comput Human
Behav. 2016;61:478-487. doi:10.1016/j.chb.2016.03.038.
Summary: Validated 9-item SMDS; found 4.2% of Dutch adolescents met ≥5/9 criterion.

### Andreassen et al. 2016
Andreassen CS, Pallesen S, Griffiths MD. The relationship between addictive use of social media,
narcissism, and self-esteem. Addict Behav. 2017;64:287-293. doi:10.1016/j.addbeh.2016.03.006.
Summary: Bergen Social Media Addiction Scale validation; 5.3% prevalence in Norwegian adults.

### Huang 2022
Huang C. A meta-analysis of the problematic social media use and mental health. Int J Soc Psychiatry.
2022;68(1):12-33. doi:10.1177/0020764020984868. PMID: 33455490.
Summary: 70 studies meta-analysed; robust associations with depression (r=0.20), anxiety (r=0.16),
loneliness (r=0.14); moderate effect sizes.

### Valkenburg et al. 2022
Valkenburg PM, et al. Social media use and its impact on adolescent mental health: An umbrella review
of the evidence. Curr Opin Psychol. 2022;44:201-206. doi:10.1016/j.copsyc.2021.09.010.
Summary: Umbrella review — associations depend heavily on type of use (active vs. passive) and
individual vulnerability factors; average effects are small.
